> *"And let us consider and give attentive, continuous care to watching over one another, studying how we may stir up to love and helpful deeds and noble activities, not forsaking or neglecting to assemble together as believers, as is the habit of some people, but admonishing one another, and all the more faithfully as you see the day approaching."*
> — Hebrews 10:24–25 (AMPC)

## The Day Is Approaching

There is a phrase tucked at the end of Hebrews 10:25 that changes the way we read everything before it: *"all the more faithfully as you see the day approaching."*

The day. The return of Christ. The end of the age. The writer of Hebrews assumes his readers can see it coming — not the exact hour, but the unmistakable shape of it on the horizon. And he writes to a church that will live in that gathering shadow until the Lord returns.

That is us. We live in those days.

And in days like these, the question is not *how do we survive?* The question is *how do we love each other?* Because the world will get colder. Hearts will grow harder. *"In the last days... the love of most will grow cold,"* Jesus warned. The temptation in cold seasons is to pull our coats tighter and look out for ourselves. But Hebrews 10 says the opposite. As the dark grows, the lights of the church must draw closer together. As the world cools, the family of God must get warmer.

That is what it means to *bless one another in these last days*. And the Bible gives us three movements to do it.

## Keep On Loving One Another

Before we get to the three movements, the foundation. *"Keep on loving one another as brothers and sisters"* (Hebrews 13:1). Five short words in English, but they hold the weight of the whole letter.

*Keep on.* Not start. Not try. *Keep on.* That phrase assumes you have been loving each other already, and the Lord is asking you not to stop. There will be reasons to stop. Misunderstandings. Offenses. Seasons when the brother annoys you and the sister disappoints you. *Keep on loving anyway.* The mark of a church that is ready for the return of Christ is not theological precision or perfect attendance. It is persistent, stubborn, family-style love.

That is the soil. Now here is what grows from it.

## 1. Consider One Another Attentively

*"And let us consider and give attentive, continuous care to watching over one another, studying how we may stir up to love and helpful deeds and noble activities"* (Hebrews 10:24, AMPC).

Read that verse slowly and notice the words the Amplified translation chose. *Consider. Attentive. Continuous. Watching. Studying.* These are not casual words. These are the words you would use for a parent watching a sick child, or a doctor reading a chart, or a student preparing for an exam they cannot afford to fail.

That is the kind of attention God asks us to pay to one another in the church.

To *consider* a brother is to think about him on purpose — to notice what is happening in his life, to remember what he asked for prayer about, to see the heaviness in his face before he tells you. To *study* a sister is to learn what stirs her up to good — what encourages her, what discourages her, what specific word the Lord might want you to bring her this week.

Most of us know how to greet each other on Sunday. *"Kumusta? Mabuti naman."* What Hebrews calls us to is something deeper. It is the difference between a passing greeting and a watchful love. Between *I saw you* and *I am studying how to bless you.*

And the goal of this attention is not just connection. It is provocation. *Stir up.* The Greek word is *paroxysmos* — the word we get *paroxysm* from. A sharp, deliberate prompting. We are meant to provoke one another — not to anger, but to love. To good deeds. To noble activities. To the kind of life that looks like Jesus.

Who are you considering this week? Whose life have you studied closely enough to know how to stir them up?

## 2. Come Together Consistently

*"Not forsaking or neglecting to assemble together as believers, as is the habit of some people"* (Hebrews 10:25a, AMPC).

The writer of Hebrews already had a problem in his church: *some people* had a habit of not coming. They had reasons, surely. They always do. Work. Tiredness. A small offense that grew. A season of drifting. The writer does not name their reasons. He just calls them out. *Some people forsake the assembly. Do not be like them.*

Coming together consistently is a spiritual discipline. It is not extra credit. It is not a preference for those who happen to enjoy church. It is a command — because the Christian life was never designed to be lived alone.

A coal taken out of the fire grows cold within minutes. Put it back in the pile, and it burns again. The gathered church is the fire. *"Iniwan ko sandali yung apoy, lumamig na agad."* You cannot maintain spiritual heat by yourself. You need the brother next to you who is also burning. You need the sister whose worship pulls you back into the room. You need the preached Word, the shared bread, the heard prayer of someone who knew you before you walked in tired.

This is why showing up matters more in cold seasons, not less. When the world feels heaviest is when *some people* drift. And drifting in the last days is dangerous. *Lalo na,* Hebrews says — *all the more,* especially now, *as you see the day approaching.*

If you have been drifting, this is the morning to come back. The fire is still burning. The church has been waiting for you.

## 3. Carry the Presence of God Faithfully

*"But admonishing one another, and all the more faithfully as you see the day approaching"* (Hebrews 10:25b, AMPC).

The Filipino translation lands with weight: *"Palakasin natin ang loob ng isa't isa, lalo na, na inyong nakikita na nalalapit na ang araw"* (Hebreo 10:24–25, SND). *Strengthen one another's hearts — all the more, because you see the day is drawing near.*

That is the third movement. We do not just *consider* one another. We do not just *gather* with one another. We *carry the presence of God* to one another.

The Amplified translation uses three words for what we do when we come together: *admonishing, warning, urging, encouraging.* Sometimes the brother needs admonishment — a loving word that says, *that path you are on is not the way of Jesus, please turn around.* Sometimes the sister needs warning — *be careful, the enemy is closer than you think.* Sometimes a friend needs urging — *do not give up, hold on, the Lord is faithful.* And sometimes someone just needs encouraging — *I see what God is doing in you, and I am for you.*

All four are forms of the same thing: carrying the presence of God to a soul that needs it. We are not just members of the same congregation. We are postmen of heaven, delivering grace to one another in person.

And this — Hebrews insists — must be done *all the more faithfully* in the last days. The closer we get to the day, the louder the urging must become. The bolder the encouraging. The kinder the warning. Because the stakes are real, and the time is short, and the brother beside us may need a word from God this week that only we are positioned to bring.

## Acts 2:42 — The Pattern That Holds

This is why our church takes its name from Acts 2:42. *"They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and to fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to prayer."* Four devotions. Not preferences. *Devotions.* The early church did not gather when it was convenient. They devoted themselves. And out of that devotion, the Lord added to their number daily those who were being saved.

Hebrews 10 is the apostolic echo of Acts 2:42. Same call. Same command. *Be devoted to one another. Watch over each other. Gather without fail. Carry the presence of God faithfully — especially now.*

The world will not get warmer on its own. The church will. And the church gets warmer one considered brother, one consistent gathering, one carried word at a time.

## Challenge

Pick one person in this church this week — not the one you are already close to, but the one the Holy Spirit just brought to your mind — and do all three things for them.

**Consider** them: think about their life on purpose. Ask the Lord what is happening in them right now that you might not see on Sunday. Study them — what would actually stir them up to love and good deeds this week?

**Come** to them: do not wait for next Sunday. Send the message. Make the call. Show up at their door if you can. Bring yourself into their life, in person, before the week is over.

**Carry** the presence of God to them: bring a word. A scripture. A prayer prayed out loud over them. An honest *I see you, I am for you, the Lord is with you.* Whatever they need — admonish, warn, urge, or encourage — carry it as if it came from the throne of heaven, because it did.

If every member of Acts 242 did this once a week, our whole church would feel the warmth of it before the year ends. And the world around us would feel it too.

**Lalo na, lalo na, na nakikita nating nalalapit na ang araw.**
