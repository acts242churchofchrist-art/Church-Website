> *"Let us rejoice and be glad and give him glory! For the wedding of the Lamb has come, and his bride has made herself ready. Fine linen, bright and clean, was given her to wear."*
> — Revelation 19:7–8

## The Wedding Has Already Been Arranged

There is a wedding at the end of the Bible.

Not a metaphor. Not a poetic flourish. A wedding. A Groom who has been waiting since before the foundation of the world, a Bride who has been chosen and bought and sealed, and a Father who has set the day. Heaven is preparing the supper. The fine linen is being woven from the righteous acts of God's holy people. And when John saw it in his vision, the only response heaven knew how to make was rejoicing: *"Let us rejoice and be glad and give him glory! For the wedding of the Lamb has come, and his bride has made herself ready"* (Revelation 19:7–9).

That Bride is the Church. That Bride is us. And whether we feel ready or not, the wedding is coming.

To understand what God is doing in our lives right now, we have to understand the wedding He is preparing us for. So let us walk through it the way a first-century Jew would have understood it — in three stages — and then ask ourselves the question the whole sermon turns on: *what does the Bride do while she waits for the Groom?*

## Stage One — The Betrothal

In ancient Jewish custom, a wedding did not begin at the ceremony. It began at the *betrothal*. The father of the groom would arrange the marriage and pay a *bride price* to the father of the bride, sealing the covenant with a signed contract called the *ketubah* and a shared cup of wine. From that moment on, the bride and groom were legally bound to each other — even though they would not yet live together. Breaking a betrothal required a divorce. The covenant was that binding.

This is exactly what God the Father has done for us.

The bride price was the most extravagant ever paid: the life of His own Son. Paul writes to the Corinthians, *"Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore honor God with your bodies"* (1 Corinthians 6:19–20). We belong to Christ now — purchased, paid for, no longer our own. Paul tells the Ephesian elders that this same Church was *"bought with his own blood"* (Acts 20:28). Not silver. Not gold. The very blood of the Son of God was the bride price.

And when we believed the gospel, the betrothal was sealed: *"And you also were included in Christ when you heard the message of truth, the gospel of your salvation. When you believed, you were marked in him with a seal, the promised Holy Spirit"* (Ephesians 1:13). The Holy Spirit living inside us is not a metaphor. He is the engagement ring. He is the seal on the contract. He is the Father's guarantee that the wedding is coming.

If you have given your life to Christ, this is your reality right now: you are already betrothed to the Lamb.

## Stage Two — The Interval

After the betrothal, the groom went home.

This part surprises modern readers. He did not stay with the bride. He returned to his father's house — sometimes for a year or longer — to *prepare a place* for her. He would add rooms onto his father's home, build the dwelling where their life together would begin, and only return for her once everything was ready. The father, not the groom, decided when the home was finished. The bride did not know the day or the hour. She just waited.

Listen to how Jesus echoed that custom on the night before He died: *"My Father's house has many rooms; if that were not so, would I have told you that I am going there to prepare a place for you? And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come back and take you to be with me that you also may be where I am"* (John 14:2–4).

He was speaking the language of a betrothed groom leaving to prepare a home. *"I go to prepare a place for you. I will come back. I will take you to be with me."* That is wedding language. That is the language of a covenant that has been signed and a return that has been promised.

We are living in that interval right now. The Groom is preparing the home. The Bride is preparing herself.

## Stage Three — The Procession

When the home was finally ready — when the father gave the word — the groom did not send a letter. He did not announce a date. He went out at night, with his friends, with torches and lamps lit, in a noisy joyful procession through the streets, to claim his bride. She heard them coming before she saw them. The whole village heard them coming. And then he took her, in celebration, back to his father's house where the feast was prepared.

This is exactly how Jesus described His return: *"Then will appear the sign of the Son of Man in heaven. And then all the peoples of the earth will mourn when they see the Son of Man coming on the clouds of heaven, with power and great glory. And he will send his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of the heavens to the other"* (Matthew 24:30–31).

The Groom is coming back the way Jewish grooms have always come back — at an hour His Bride does not know, with a sound the whole earth will hear, in a procession of clouds and angels and the trumpet of God. The procession will not be quiet. The whole world will know the wedding has come.

## What Does the Bride Do While Waiting for the Groom?

This is the question every Christian must answer with their life. The betrothal is settled — the Father has paid the bride price, the Son has gone to prepare the place, the Spirit has sealed the contract on our hearts. The procession is coming — we cannot stop it, hurry it, or delay it. The only thing left for us is the *interval*. And in the interval, the Bride does three things.

### 1. The Bride Keeps Herself Pure

*"I am jealous for you with a godly jealousy. I promised you to one husband, to Christ, so that I might present you as a pure virgin to him"* (2 Corinthians 11:2).

Paul says it the only way a faithful friend of the Groom could say it: *I am jealous for you.* Not possessive. Jealous *for* — wanting Christ to receive what was promised Him, which is a Bride still pure on the day He comes for her.

Purity is not just a sexual word. It is a whole-life word. The Bride keeps her heart pure. Her eyes pure. Her speech pure. Her ambitions pure. Not because she is afraid of being caught, but because she is *promised* to Someone else. Her body, her time, her affection — none of it is hers to give to the world anymore. It already belongs to the One who paid for it.

If we have given our hearts to Christ, then everything we do this week is being done in front of the Groom who will return.

### 2. The Bride Must Be Faithful

*"You adulterous people, don't you know that friendship with the world means enmity against God? Therefore, anyone who chooses to be a friend of the world becomes an enemy of God"* (James 4:4).

James does not use soft language. He calls divided affection what it is: adultery. The Bride who is *promised to Christ* but flirts with the world is not just being foolish — she is being unfaithful. She is signaling to the Groom that she is not sure she actually wants Him.

Friendship with the world looks different in each life. For one it is the relationship she will not let go of even though she knows it is leading her away from God. For another it is the ambition that has become more important than obedience. For another it is the entertainment she keeps feeding her soul that quietly corrodes her love for Jesus. The world has a hundred subtle invitations to friendship. James calls every one of them what they are: an enemy of God.

The Bride must be faithful. Not perfect — but faithful. When she falls, she runs back to her Groom and not to her old life.

### 3. The Bride Must Be Ready Anytime

*"Therefore keep watch, because you do not know on what day your Lord will come. But understand this: If the owner of the house had known at what time of night the thief was coming, he would have kept watch and would not have let his house be broken into. So you also must be ready, because the Son of Man will come at an hour when you do not expect him"* (Matthew 24:42–44).

This is the third thing — and the hardest. The Bride does not know the day. She does not know the hour. She cannot mark a calendar and live carelessly until the week before. She must live *every day* as a day He might come.

Paul wrote to the Philippians, *"Therefore, my dear friends, as you have always obeyed — not only in my presence, but now much more in my absence — continue to work out your salvation with fear and trembling"* (Philippians 2:12). The Bride does not work out her salvation more carefully when the preacher is watching. She works it out when no one is watching, because she knows the Groom might come at the exact moment she stops watching.

That is not a fearful way to live. It is a clarifying way to live. Every choice becomes a wedding-day choice. Every word becomes a wedding-day word. Every secret habit becomes a wedding-day habit. We are not just killing time until Jesus comes. We are getting ready for the day we see Him.

## The Home He Has Prepared

The reason we wait this way — the reason the purity, the faithfulness, the readiness — is what is waiting on the other side of the procession.

*"Then I saw 'a new heaven and a new earth,' for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and there was no longer any sea. I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband. And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, 'Look! God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God. He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away'"* (Revelation 21:1–4).

This is the home. This is what He has been preparing all along. A city coming down out of heaven, a Bride beautifully dressed, a God who dwells with His people forever. No more death. No more mourning. No more tears wiped away with the back of a tired hand — God Himself will reach out and wipe them. The old order is finished. The wedding has come.

That is what we are waiting for. That is what makes the interval worth living well.

## Challenge

This week, ask yourself one question every morning before your feet hit the floor: *if today is the day the Groom returns, am I ready?*

Not perfect. Not flawless. *Ready.* Pure in the places you have control over. Faithful in the affections you have given to lesser things. Watching with the kind of attention that does not get distracted by a long interval.

If your answer is *yes* — even a trembling yes — then go into the day with joy, because the wedding is closer today than it was yesterday. If your answer is *no, not yet* — then this is the morning to make it ready. Repent of the friendship with the world. Surrender the affection you have been holding back. Wash the linen in the blood of the Lamb. The Groom does not require you to be perfect. He requires you to be *His*.

He is coming. Soon. And blessed are those who are invited to the wedding supper of the Lamb.

**The Bridegroom is on His way home.**
