> *"There remains, then, a Sabbath-rest for the people of God; for anyone who enters God's rest also rests from his own works, just as God did from his. Let us, therefore, make every effort to enter that rest, so that no one will fall by following their example of disobedience."*
> — Hebrews 4:9–11

## The Edge of the Promised Land

There is a generation in the Bible that walked all the way out of Egypt, through the parted sea, across the wilderness — and then refused to enter the land God had promised them.

They had seen miracles most of us will never see. They had eaten bread that fell from heaven and drunk water that came out of a rock. They had watched a pillar of fire guide them through the dark. And when they finally stood at the edge of the Promised Land, with the new normal sitting right in front of them, they looked at it and said no.

The writer of Hebrews looks back at that generation and asks a question that should make every modern believer go quiet for a moment. *Why?* Why did they refuse the rest God had prepared for them?

The answer is the entire sermon. *"They were not able to enter, because of their unbelief"* (Hebrews 3:19). And the warning that follows is sharper still: *"Therefore, since the promise of entering his rest still stands, let us be careful that none of you be found to have fallen short of it"* (Hebrews 4:1). The same rest is open today. The same unbelief still keeps people out.

This morning we ask the question Hebrews is asking. *Are we entering God's rest, or are we still looking back at Egypt?*

## What Is God's Rest?

Before we can enter it, we have to understand it.

In the Bible, *God's rest* signifies much more than taking a physical break. It is not a Sunday afternoon nap. It is not a vacation from work. It is a spiritual state — the state of having stopped trying to earn what God has already given, trusting fully in the finished work of Jesus, and entering into a peace that does not depend on circumstances.

The word *rest* appears eleven times in this passage in Hebrews. Eleven. The writer wants to make sure we cannot miss it.

He grounds the idea all the way back at creation: *"For somewhere he has spoken about the seventh day in these words: 'And on the seventh day God rested from all his work'"* (Hebrews 4:4). God rested not because He was tired, but because the work was finished. The seventh day was the declaration that creation was complete.

That is what God's rest still is for us. *"Anyone who enters God's rest also rests from his own works, just as God did from his"* (Hebrews 4:10). We stop striving to earn salvation. We stop trying to add anything to the cross. We stop performing for the approval of God that has already been freely given. The work is done. The only thing left for us is to enter.

And the door is still open: *"There remains, then, a Sabbath-rest for the people of God"* (Hebrews 4:9). *Today*, the writer says — quoting David, who quoted God: *"Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts"* (Hebrews 4:7).

The invitation has not expired. The question is whether we will accept it.

## Why the Israelites Missed It

To understand why we miss God's rest, we have to look at why they missed it.

*"And to whom did God swear that they would never enter his rest if not to those who disobeyed? So we see that they were not able to enter, because of their unbelief"* (Hebrews 3:18–19).

Two words. *Disobedience* and *unbelief*. They go together. Unbelief is the root; disobedience is the fruit. When the heart no longer trusts God, the hands no longer obey Him.

And what makes this story so painful is that God had already promised them everything they needed. Read what He said to them through Moses before they ever left Egypt: *"I am the Lord, and I will bring you out from under the yoke of the Egyptians. I will free you from being slaves to them, and I will redeem you with an outstretched arm and with mighty acts of judgment. I will take you as my own people, and I will be your God. Then you will know that I am the Lord your God, who brought you out from under the yoke of the Egyptians. And I will bring you to the land I swore with uplifted hand to give to Abraham, to Isaac and to Jacob"* (Exodus 6:6–8).

Look at the verbs. *I will bring out. I will free. I will redeem. I will take. I will be. I will bring to the land.* Six promises in three sentences. Every single thing they needed was already promised. The freedom was already done. The land was already theirs. The God of the covenant was already with them.

And they still did not enter.

## They Could Not Recognize the New Normal

Here is the heart of the diagnosis. They kept looking back at the abnormal life of Egypt — the slavery, the idols, the food chain they had been part of — and because they kept looking back, they could not see the new normal God had set in front of them.

There were three patterns in their refusal, and every one of them shows up in our lives too.

**They wanted to remain slaves and be fed by the Egyptians.** Slavery sounds horrifying until you have known nothing else. The Israelites had a strange comfort in their chains — at least in Egypt, somebody handed them their food. The wilderness asked them to trust God for every meal, and they hated it. *"Bigyan na lang kami ng dating buhay. At least may kanin doon."* The bondage felt safer than the trust.

**They still wanted to worship idols.** Even after the burning bush, even after the plagues, even after the parting of the sea, they wanted the golden calf. Their hearts had not yet caught up with their feet. They were physically out of Egypt. Egypt was not yet out of them.

**They did not want to walk in faith.** The whole journey from the Red Sea to the Promised Land was a faith walk. No map. No timeline. No guarantees about tomorrow's bread. And they hated walking that way. They wanted certainty. They wanted control. They wanted Google Maps, and God kept giving them a compass.

These three refusals are why an entire generation died in the wilderness within sight of the rest they had been promised. Not because God closed the door. Because they would not walk through it.

## The New Normal for Us

So what is the new normal for us today? What is it that we are being invited into?

Jesus answered the question in one verse: *"So if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed"* (John 8:36). That is the new normal. Real freedom. Not partial freedom. Not freedom on a leash. Freedom *indeed* — the kind that goes all the way down to the roots of the soul.

The cross paid for it. The empty tomb sealed it. The Holy Spirit applies it to anyone who comes by faith. And the moment we believe, we are no longer slaves of Egypt. We are children of God, with full access to the rest God prepared from the foundation of the world.

But here is where we have to be honest. Most of us are like the Israelites. We are physically out of Egypt. We are not yet living like Egypt is behind us.

## Four Ways We Look Back at the Abnormal

If we are honest, there are four common ways we keep looking back when God is calling us forward.

**First, we fail to trust the mercy of God which is new every morning.** Lamentations says His mercies are new every morning — fresh, fully sufficient, custom-fit for the day in front of us. But we keep waking up and replaying yesterday's failure. We carry guilt that God already forgave. We rehearse sins God already forgot. We treat our past like a leash long enough to choke us, when the truth is the leash was cut at the cross. *"Hindi na ako ang dating ako."* That is the new normal. Anything else is looking back.

**Second, we accept the bondage of poverty.** This is not about the prosperity gospel. This is about identity. The world tells us we are limited by what we can earn. The Bible tells us we are heirs of God and co-heirs with Christ, that He owns the cattle on a thousand hills, and that every good and perfect gift comes down from the Father. Some of us have accepted a poverty mindset — *I will never have enough, I cannot give, I cannot bless, I cannot dream* — when our Father is the wealthiest Being in the universe. The bondage of poverty is not always about how much money is in your bank. It is about whether you actually believe God provides.

**Third, we accept the bondage of sickness.** Again, this is not the cheap promise that no Christian ever gets sick. Bodies break. Cells multiply wrong. The world is groaning. But Christ's healing is real, and many of us never even ask for it. We accept the diagnosis as the final word when God is the One who has the final word. We pray for everyone else's healing and quietly assume nothing will change for us. *Walang nakakabuti rito, baka tinakdaan ako.* That whisper of resignation is a looking-back. The new normal is to pray boldly, ask boldly, trust the Healer — and then submit to whatever He decides, knowing He is good.

**Fourth, we live as if this world is our permanent home.** This is the deepest version of looking back. We build our whole lives — our careers, our houses, our retirement plans, our identities — as if the wilderness is where the story ends. But the Bible says we are foreigners and exiles passing through. Our home is not here. The rest God is preparing for us is not a comfortable life in this world; it is an eternity of His presence in the world to come. When we forget that, we cling too tightly to what we have and we miss the rest He is offering.

These four are our Egypt. They are the abnormal we keep glancing back at. And the longer we glance back, the longer we wander in a wilderness God never intended for us to camp in.

## Today, If You Hear His Voice

The writer of Hebrews gives one final word, and it is meant for this morning, not for some other morning when we feel ready. *"Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts"* (Hebrews 4:7).

Today. Not tomorrow. Not when the schedule clears. Not when the relationship heals. Not when the bank account recovers. *Today.* The door to God's rest is open right now, and the only thing keeping us out is whatever we are still holding onto from Egypt.

*"Let us, therefore, make every effort to enter that rest, so that no one will fall by following their example of disobedience"* (Hebrews 4:11). Notice the strange phrase — *make every effort to enter rest*. Rest takes effort? Yes. The effort is the surrender. The effort is the letting go. The effort is the refusing to look back. The effort is choosing, every single morning, to walk forward in faith instead of camping out in unbelief.

## Challenge

This week, name your Egypt.

Pick the one place where you are still looking back. The guilt you keep rehearsing. The poverty mindset that says God will not provide. The sickness you have stopped praying about. The way you are quietly building your life as if this world is the destination.

Then do one concrete thing to step forward.

Forgive yourself the way God already forgave you. Give an offering that costs you something, as a declaration that you trust the Provider. Pray for the healing you have stopped asking for. Make a decision this week that only makes sense if heaven is real.

Each step is small. But each step is a step out of the wilderness and into the rest. And the One who leads you out is the same One who has been waiting for you to come home this whole time.

**Today, if you hear His voice, do not harden your hearts.**
